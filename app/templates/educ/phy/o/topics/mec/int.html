Introduction To Light


Light

Light is a form of energy which is responsible for the sense of sight. Light belongs to a family of electromagnetic waves. Electromagnetic waves do not need a medium fro their transmission hence light can travel through a vacuum and the speed of light in a vacuum is 3 x 108 ms-1.


Sources of Light:

An object is only seen if light reflected from the object enters our eyes. Most of the objects do not make their own light but reflect light from other sources to our eyes. The following are sources of light;

  • Luminous Objects

    These are objects which produce their own light. Examples are the sun, stars, glow warms, electric bulbs, among others. Some of these luminous objects are natural and others are artificial.

  • Non-Luminous Objects

    These are objects which do not produce their own light. Examples are the moon, the earth, among others. These non-luminous objects just reflect light from other sources and they appear as though, they are producing the light. The moon is the perfect example because it reflects light from the sun to the earth.

  • Incandescent Objects

    These are objects which give off light when they are hot. Examples are fire wood, candles, electric filaments, metallic objects, among others. When these objects are burning or when they their temperatures go beyond certain limits, they produce light.

  • Fluorescent Objects

    These are luminous objects that produce light without being hot. Examples are glow warms, stars, among others. They are naturally occurring objects which do not need heat to produce light.

  • Phosphorescent Objects

    These are objects that absorb the incident energy falling on them and emit energy in form of light later. Examples are calcium sulphide, among others.

Note;

A body seen in light of other luminous bodies is referred to as an illuminated body.


Transmission of Light

Light travels from its source to another point through a vacuum or a medium. There are three media through which light travels and these are;

  • Transparent Medium

    This the type of medium which allows almost all the light to pass through it and objects are seen clearly. Examples are glass, pure or distilled water, paraffin, among others. Incident light on these media just passes through after being bent to other different angles.

  • Translucent Medium

    This is the type of medium which allows some of the light to pass through it and objects are not seen clearly. These include frosted glass, oily paper, cloudy liquids, among others. Most of the light incident on these media is absorbed and the little that goes through not enough to make the objects clear.

  • Opaque Medium

    This is the type of medium which does not allow light to pass through it and objects are not seen at all. These media absorb all the light incident on them. Examples are walls, stones, among others.

Rays and Beams

A Ray

A ray of light is the direction or the path along which light travels. A ray of light is represented by a straight line with an arrow showing the direction in which light is traveling.

A collection of rays is called a beam. There are three types of beams and these are;

  • Parallel Beam

  • Convergent Beam

  • Divergent Beam

Parallel Beam:

This s a collection of rays of light which do not meet or intersect. Examples of sources of such beams include the search lights, among others.

Convergent Beam:

This is a collection of rays originating from different directions and meeting at one point. Examples of sources of such beams are hand lens, among others.

Divergent Beam:

This is a collection of rays originating from one point and traveling in different directions. The examples of sources that produce such beams include, the torch, electrical bulbs, car head lamp, among others.

Rectilinear Propagation of Light

This is a process by which light travels in a straight line when produced from its source. When it meats an obstacle like an opaque object, it is absorbed, hover, when it meets a transparent or translucent object, some of it or all of it goes through after some bending. When it meets a smooth surface like a plane mirror, or a polished body, the rays bounce off into another direction.

Rectilinear propagation of light leads to the formation of Shadows and Eclipses.

Shadows:

A shadow is an area where light cannot reach. Therefore, a shadow is formed when an opaque object obstructs the light because light travels in a straight line.

A shadow formed by a Point source of Light

A point source of light can be obtained by placing a cardboard with a small hole in front of a lamp. When an opaque object is laced between the screen and the point source of light, a shadow is formed on the screen as shown below. The shadow is totally dark and total darkness is called Umbra.


A shadow formed by an Extended source of Light

When a cardboard is removed from the front of the lamp, it becomes an extended light source. Two regions of the shadow are formed when an extended source of light is used. These regions are Umbra and Penumbra. Umbra is a region of a shadow where no light reaches at all while Penumbra is a region of a shadow where some light reaches.

Eclipses:

An eclipse occurs when the sun, the moon and the earth are in a straight line. There are two types of eclipses and these are;

  • Solar Eclipse (Eclipse of The Sun)

  • Lunar Eclipse (Eclipse of The Moon)


Solar Eclipse:

This occurs when the moon is between the sun and the earth so that both the Umbra and the Penumbra reach the Earth. This is because the moon revolves around the Earth, and the Earth revolves around the Sun. It is sometimes referred to as the eclipse of the Sun.

In a Solar Eclipse, areas on Earth fall under the moon's shadow and two shadows are formed on earth, that is, Umbra which is formed when the moon blocks the Sun completely and the Sun is not seen at all and Penumbra which is formed when the moon blocks the Sun partly and part of the Sun can be seen. Umbra covers a very small area compared to Penumbra which covers a large area hence very many people on Earth experience Partial Eclipse rather than Total Eclipse of the Sun. In a solar Eclipse, the area covered by the Umbra has total Darkness and the Sun cannot be seen at all, while the areas covered by the Penumbra has partial darkness and a part of the Sun can be seen.


Types of Solar Eclipses

  • Partial Solar Eclipse

    This occurs when the Moon and the Sun do not come in the same straight line but the Moon partly covers the sun hence the Sun is visible from a large portion of the Earth. In this type of eclipse, the Umbra of the Moon completely misses and therefore no region on Earth will experience total solar eclipse but only part of the Penumbra covers some regions on the Earth.

  • Total Solar Eclipse

    In this type of eclipse, the Moon looks as big as the Sun hence covering the Sun completely and this happens when the Earth and the Moon are closest to each other. Total eclipse is observed by only people in the Umbra region.

  • Annular Solar Eclipse

    This occurs when the Sun and the Moon are positioned in one straight line but the Moon does not completely cover the Sun. In this case, the Moon appears smaller than the Sun and a bright ring is visible in the sky. This happens when the Earth is furthest from the Moon so that the Umbra does not reach the earth but only the Penumbra reaches the Earth.


Note:

Total Solar eclipse is a rare event that on an average it would repeat itself only once in about 370 years. The duration of a Total Eclipse is really very short that it can not last for more than 7 minutes and 30 seconds. This is because, the Moon's Umbra is moving at a speed of about 1,700kmh-1 across the Earth's surface. It is estimated that the longest Solar eclipse will occur on July 16, 2186, and will last for a record of 7 minutes and 29 seconds only. One of the longest Solar Eclipse of the 21st century occurred on July 22, 2013, lasting only 6 minutes and 39 seconds.

Lunar Eclipse:

This occurs when the Earth is between the Sun and the Moon. It is sometimes referred to as the Eclipse of the Moon. In Lunar Eclipse, the shadow of the Earth is formed on the Moon. Lunar eclipse happens when the Moon passes through the Earth's shadow. The Earth always has a shadow created by the Sun and this shadow is composed of Two portions, that is, the Penumbra where some portion of Sun's light rays are blocked and the Umbra where all the Sun's light rays are blocked.

The Eclipse of the Moon lasts longer than the Eclipse of the Sun because the earth is larger than the Moon in the eclipse of the Sun.

  • Partial Lunar Eclipse

    This occurs when the portion of the moon passes through Earth's Umbra shadow, this event can easily be seen.

  • Total Lunar Eclipse

    This occurs when the entire moon passes through the Earth's Umbra shadow, during this event, the color of the Moon changes and it takes about 90 minutes.

  • Penumbral Lunar Eclipse

    This occurs when the Moon passes through the Earth's Penumbra shadow, this event is quite difficult to observe because the moon's light is dimmed but does not go dark since the Penumbra show is not dark enough to block the Sun's light rays. This is some times referred to as a Pulse Eclipse.

Pinhole Camera

A Pinhole Camera is a simple camera without a lens and with a single small aperture (a pinhole) that is a light-proof box with a small hole in one side. It consists of a light tight box with a hole on one side and a screen on the opposite where the image is formed. Light from a scene passes through this single point and projects an inverted image on the opposite side of the box. When the object is placed in front of a pinhole camera, light rays from the object passes through the pinhole un-deviated and a real, diminished and inverted image is formed on the screen at the back of the pinhole camera.

A pinhole camera is completely dark on all the other sides of the box including the side where the pinhole is created. This part is usually painted black, but black boxes are also used for this purpose. There is also a thin screen which looks like a projector sheet, and is put in between the ark side adjacent to the pin-hole.

Effect On Image Formation In A Pin-Hole Camera

  • When the object is moved closer to the pinhole camera, the size of the image increases but becomes less bright because the same amount of light is now spread over a large area of the image.

  • WHen the pinhole is enlarged, the image becomes blurred (not clear) and more bright because a large hole will be the same as many pinholes put together each forming its image.

  • When the distance between the pinhole and the screen is increased, the size of the image increases and the image becomes less bright because the same amount of light is now spread over a large are of the image.
